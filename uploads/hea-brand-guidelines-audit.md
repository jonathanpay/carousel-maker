# Holistic Email Academy — Reverse-Engineered Brand Guidelines

*Derived from site audit of holisticemailacademy.com. March 2026.*

---

## 1. Colour Palette

### Primary Colours

| Name | Hex | Usage observed |
|---|---|---|
| Academy Purple | #9E41BF | Dominant brand colour. Buttons, CTAs, accents throughout |
| Soft Purple / Accent | #AF81BF | Secondary accent, softer supporting tone |
| Off-White / Background | #FFFFFF | Primary page background |
| Deep Plum / Text | #3D2A35 | Body text, headings — a warm, dark purple-brown |

### Difficulty / Level Indicators

| Level | Colour | Hex |
|---|---|---|
| Beginner | Green | #21BF3D |
| Intermediate | Cyan | #22CEF2 |
| Advanced | Purple | #9E41BF |

The use of a colour-coded level system is a notable brand asset. It signals accessibility and structured progression — two values the brand overtly claims.

### Palette Character

The palette reads warm and authoritative rather than clinical or techy. The purple dominant is unusual in a B2B education context (most lean blue or green), and it gives HEA a distinctive, slightly aspirational quality — not corporate, not startup-casual. The warm plum body text reinforces that positioning: it feels considered, not default.

---

## 2. Typography

**Primary typeface: Montserrat** (Google Fonts). Used throughout the site — headings, body, navigation, CTAs.

Montserrat is a geometric sans-serif with humanist warmth. It reads as clean and modern without the coldness of something like Helvetica or the neutrality of Inter. It suits the "accessible expert" positioning well — authoritative enough for a professional education brand, approachable enough not to feel corporate.

**Typographic principles observed:**

- Clean hierarchy: clear distinction between H1, H2, H3, and body
- Italic is used meaningfully — subheadings, quotes, taglines — suggesting it carries tonal weight
- Bold is used liberally for emphasis in body copy (more so than would be considered restrained)
- Short-form type (taglines, CTAs) is punchy and often fragment-based

## 3. Logo & Mark

The full wordmark includes both "Holistic" and "Email Academy" — the brand carries the parent company's name but with "Academy" appended to signal the education-focused sub-brand. The logo proportions suggest a wide, horizontal lock-up.

No standalone icon or monogram is visible from the site. The brand currently relies on the full wordmark in all contexts.

## 4. Visual Style

### Imagery

The site uses a mix of:
- Illustrated/icon-style character illustrations (digital marketers, email specialists, CRM managers) — flat, clean, tile-format
- Noun Project-style iconography in white on purple for the About page value propositions
- Stock photography on blog posts — editorial/ambient rather than product-focused
- Headshots for people (Kath Pay) — real, not illustrated

### Character

Warm but not playful. Professional but approachable. There's a slight tension between the authority-signalling content and the somewhat generic stock photography, which feels like a growth area.

## 5. Tone of Voice

### Core Character

The brand voice is **authoritative, warm, and direct**. It has a clear mission and isn't shy about it. There's a recurring sense that HEA exists in opposition to something — poor education, inaccessible pricing, tool-led thinking — and that tension gives the copy energy.

### Dominant Patterns

**Declarative confidence.** Short, standalone sentences used as anchors. The site opens with "Email marketing is complex." on the About page — no softening, no build-up. The confidence is the signal.

**Challenge-led framing.** Headlines and body copy frequently identify a problem before offering the solution. "You Don't Have an Email Strategy. You Have a To-Do List." "Most email marketing education out there focuses on tools, not on the thinking behind the send." This is a consistent rhetorical move.

**Punchy two or three-part rhythms.** "Stop guessing. Start growing." "No fluff. No hype. Just sharp, practical insights." "Smarter Email Starts Here." The brand defaults heavily to this structure — it's the dominant cadence across headings, taglines, and CTAs.

**The contrast construction.** "This isn't just another email marketing course." "We're not a tech vendor." "Not just a place to learn — it's a community." The brand frequently defines itself by what it is *not*. This is effective for positioning but appears often enough to be a pattern worth monitoring.

**"We" voice with mission framing.** The brand speaks as a collective with purpose. "We're here to elevate the whole industry." "We exist to support..." "We're changing that." It positions HEA as a movement, not just a course catalogue.

**Second-person directness.** Heavy use of "you" and "your" — the copy addresses the reader personally and consistently. "You're in expert hands." "You'll always find your fit."

### Recurring Vocabulary

Words and phrases that appear repeatedly and feel load-bearing for the brand:
- *Ethical* — appears across copy, signals values-alignment positioning
- *Strategic* — the consistent differentiator from tactical/tool-led thinking
- *Accessible* — tied directly to pricing (£75) and framing
- *Holistic* — inherited from the parent brand; used to mean integrated, whole-system thinking
- *Practical* — appears in almost every section as a counterweight to "theory"
- *Confidence* — the emotional outcome the brand promises ("Master Email Marketing with Confidence")
- *CPD-certified / CPD-accredited* — credential-signalling, appears everywhere

### Tone Modifiers

- Warm but not sentimental
- Ambitious without being hyperbolic (mostly — "transformative" and "revolutionise" do appear in the book description)
- Inclusive without being vague about who the audience is
- Expert without being exclusionary

### Tagline Variants in Use

Several taglines appear across the site and should probably be rationalised:

| Tagline | Location |
|---|---|
| "Smarter Email Starts Here" | Blog header, in-page heading |
| "Smarter Email Starts With You" | Footer |
| "Master Email Marketing with Confidence" | Homepage hero |
| "Stop guessing. Start growing" | Homepage subheading |
| "Smart, ethical, and intentional marketing — one send at a time" | Footer subscribe section |

There are five competing lines doing similar positioning work. The brand would benefit from choosing one primary tagline and using the others contextually rather than simultaneously.

## 6. Brand Personality — Inferred Summary

If HEA were a person:

- A senior practitioner who genuinely enjoys teaching
- Warm enough to be encouraging, but direct enough to tell you when your thinking is wrong
- Principled — they care about ethics and won't recommend shortcuts
- Accessible by design, not by accident — they've thought about who gets left out and tried to fix it
- Slightly evangelical about their methodology (in a good way)

---

## 7. Gaps & Observations

**Tagline proliferation.** Five distinct taglines is too many. Pick one. Use the others contextually or retire them.

**Contrast construction overuse.** "This isn't just..." and "We're not..." are effective, but the brand leans on them heavily. Worth varying the approach.

**Bold for emphasis is frequent.** The body copy bolds generously. This dilutes the emphasis function — when everything is emphasised, nothing is.

**Stock photography tension.** The brand's warmth and specificity are real. The generic editorial photography doesn't always match that register. Custom or more characterful photography would close that gap.

**Icon consistency.** The Noun Project-style icons work well on the About page but feel slightly at odds with the more polished illustrated characters used elsewhere. A unified illustration style would tighten the visual language.

**No standalone brand mark.** Relying solely on the wordmark limits flexibility. A symbol or monogram would help in contexts where the full wordmark is too wide (social profile images, favicons, etc.).
